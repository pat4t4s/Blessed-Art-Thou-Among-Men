LORE





BLESSED ART THOU AMONG MEN



In a forgotten village where faith and fear intertwine, whispers speak of a man named Joseph, a sinner in the eyes of many, yet a devoted husband in the eyes of God. Branded a thief, a killer, and a wretch, Joseph carries the weight of his sins not for greed, but for love. His wife, Maria, lies bedridden, afflicted by a mysterious illness that no ordinary remedy can cure.

Driven by desperation, Joseph journeys into the depths of the village and beyond, seeking medicine said to exist only in places touched by darkness. But the closer he comes to salvation, the more the world around him begins to unravel.

The village does not remain still.

Shadows stretch unnaturally. Voices echo where no one stands. The sins of Joseph’s past take form, manifesting as visions that claw at his mind. To survive, he must pray. Faith becomes his shield against hallucinations, his only defense against a reality that twists itself into something unrecognizable.

Yet as Joseph presses forward, Maria’s letters begin to change.

At first, they are filled with warmth and reassurance, her gentle words urging him onward. She speaks of hope, of faith, of her belief that he will return. But slowly, almost imperceptibly, her tone shifts. The words grow quieter, heavier. She begins to question, not Joseph, but herself. Her strength, once unwavering, begins to waver in silence.

She writes of loneliness.

Of long nights where the walls feel closer than before.

Of prayers that seem to echo back unanswered.

Still, she tells him to continue. To not lose faith.

To come back.

By the time Joseph reaches the cure, he is no longer certain what is real. The line between divine trial and personal torment has blurred beyond recognition. But he holds on to one truth: Maria is waiting.

She has to be.

When Joseph finally returns, medicine in hand, the house is silent.

Too silent.

Maria lies where he left her, untouched by the passage of time, her presence now a stillness that the room cannot escape. Beside her rests a final letter, written not in fear, but in quiet acceptance.

In it, she speaks of love.

Of gratitude.

Of release.

She assures Joseph that his journey was never in vain, but not in the way he had hoped. Her final words do not condemn him, nor do they curse his absence. Instead, they reveal a truth far more unsettling:

Not all battles are fought in the world beyond.

Some are fought alone, in silence, within.

As Joseph falls to his knees, the shadows gather once more, but this time, they do not whisper.

They listen.

Because the question is no longer whether Joseph can save Maria.

It is whether his faith can survive the truth.
